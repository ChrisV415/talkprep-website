How to Navigate a Performance Review Conversation
(Scripts for Both Sides of the Table)

Performance reviews are among the most awkward conversations in professional life — largely because neither side prepares for them properly. Here's exactly what to say, whether you're giving the review or receiving it.

Most performance reviews fail not because of what's in them, but because of how they're delivered and received. Managers bury hard feedback in softening. Employees sit passively when they disagree. Neither side leaves with clarity about what happens next.

If you're giving the review — what to say and how to open

The most important thing about giving a review: don't use the opening to summarise everything you're about to say. Open with the purpose, then move directly into the conversation.

Manager opening — clear and collaborative
"I want to use this time well, so I'm going to be direct with you. Overall, I see [summary: strengths first, then the area of focus]. The main thing I want us to dig into today is [the primary area for development], because I think it's the thing that will most affect your trajectory here.

I'd also like to hear from you — what feels accurate, what doesn't, and what you need from me."
💡 "What feels accurate, what doesn't" invites dialogue rather than a monologue. It also reduces defensiveness because it signals you're open to their perspective.

When the rating is lower than expected

Delivering a below-expectations rating
"I want to be honest with you about your rating before you see it in writing. I've rated you as [rating] this cycle. I know that may be different from what you expected, and I want to explain my reasoning and hear your reaction.

The specific areas that drove this decision were [concrete examples]. I didn't come to this lightly — I've reflected on the full picture of your work, and this reflects where I genuinely see the gaps."
💡 "I know that may be different from what you expected" names the potential gap before they have to. It shows self-awareness and reduces the shock.

If you're receiving the review — how to show up and respond

Most employees go into performance reviews passively — listening, nodding, and saying very little. This is a missed opportunity. Your review is also a negotiation: about your future, your raise, and your development. Prepare to participate actively.

Employee opening — if you have things you want to cover
"Before we dive in, can I share a few things I've been reflecting on? I want to make sure we cover [specific contribution or win] and I'd also like to talk about [a specific goal or concern]. I'm happy to hear your feedback first — I just wanted to flag those so they're on the table."
💡 Saying what you want to cover upfront signals preparation and confidence. It also ensures your wins are on the table, not just the gaps.

How to push back when you disagree with your rating

Disagreeing with a rating — professionally
"I appreciate you sharing your perspective, and I want to respond honestly — I see some of this differently. Specifically, [the area you're disputing]: I think [your evidence or context that changes the picture].

I'm not trying to argue for the sake of it. I want to understand how you're weighting these things, and I want to make sure the full picture is considered. Can we talk through this specific area?"
💡 "I'm not trying to argue for the sake of it" takes the heat out of the pushback. You're asking for a conversation, not a fight.
🤝If they don't budge on the rating

I hear what you're saying, but the rating reflects where I see the overall performance this cycle.

What to say:I understand, and I respect the decision even if I see it differently. What I'd like to take from today is a clear understanding of what 'meets expectations' looks like in your eyes — specifically — so I know exactly what I'm working toward next cycle. Can we make that concrete?

Why this works: you accept the decision without capitulating emotionally, and you immediately redirect toward the future — which is the thing you can actually influence.

How to use the review to open the salary conversation

Opening the raise conversation at review time
"I also wanted to raise something I've been thinking about — my compensation. Based on my research into market rates for this role, and looking at what I've contributed this year, I'd like to talk about a raise to [specific number].

Is this something we can discuss as part of this cycle, or should I schedule a separate conversation with you about it?"
💡 "Is this something we can discuss or should I schedule a separate conversation" gives them an easy path either way — and signals that you're going to have this conversation regardless.

The mistakes both sides make

✗
Manager: saving hard feedback for the review. Nothing in a performance review should be a surprise. If the feedback hasn't been given continuously through the year, the review isn't the right place for the first mention.
✗
Employee: passive listening. Your review is also a conversation. You're allowed — and expected — to respond, push back on things that aren't accurate, and ask what you need to do to progress.
✗
Both: ending without clear next steps. What specifically is the plan? What are the three things that matter most in the next six months? Who is responsible for what? Write it down before the conversation ends.
✗
Employee: not addressing disagreements in the moment. If you sign the review without noting your disagreement, you've tacitly agreed with it. It's appropriate to note where you see things differently, professionally and specifically, before the review is formally closed.
