How to Fire Someone
With Dignity (Exact Scripts)

The termination conversation is one of the most consequential you'll ever have as a manager. Done badly, it causes lasting damage to both people. Done well, it's honest, clear, and leaves the person with their dignity intact.

Most managers dread this conversation so much that they delay it, soften it, or avoid it altogether — which makes everything worse. The person stays in a role they're failing at. The team watches a performance problem go unaddressed. And when the termination eventually happens, it comes as a crisis rather than a conclusion.

This guide gives you the exact structure and words for the termination conversation — not to make it easy, but to make it honest, clear, and human.

78%of managers report delaying a termination they knew was necessary by at least 2 weeks
60%of terminated employees say they were surprised — meaning the feedback before wasn't clear enough
3×more likely to be legally challenged when the conversation is unclear or perceived as unfair

Before the meeting — what must be in place first

The conversation itself takes 10–20 minutes. The preparation before it determines whether it's defensible, humane, and final.

Confirm the decision has been made

The termination meeting is not the place to decide whether to fire someone. That decision should already be made, documented, and confirmed with HR and legal before you sit down. Going into the meeting with ambiguity — "we might be letting you go" — creates confusion, raises legal risk, and prolongs suffering for everyone. If you haven't confirmed the decision, postpone the meeting.

Prepare the paperwork before you speak

Have the separation agreement, final paycheck information, COBRA notice, and any severance documentation ready before the meeting starts. Nothing makes a termination feel more chaotic than the manager fumbling through paperwork or saying "we'll get you the details later." The person should leave the meeting with everything they need.

Pick the right time and setting

Do not fire someone on a Friday afternoon — it leaves them with a weekend of spiraling anxiety and no ability to take action. Tuesday or Wednesday morning is best. A private room, never at their desk or in a public space. Keep it to 15–20 minutes. Do not schedule it before a company all-hands or team event.

⚖️
Legal note. This guide covers the interpersonal and communication aspects of termination. Employment law varies significantly by state, country, and company policy. Always consult HR and legal before conducting a termination — especially for performance-based dismissals. The scripts here are frameworks, not legal advice.

How to open — the first 60 seconds

The most important thing about the opening is this: say the reason you called the meeting in the first sentence. Do not do small talk. Do not build up to it. The person likely suspects something is wrong. Prolonging the opening creates false hope and more anxiety.

Opening script — performance-based termination Most common scenario
"Thank you for coming in. I need to share some difficult news with you today. We've made the decision to end your employment, effective [date].

This decision is based on [the performance issues we've discussed over the past X months / the specific incidents we've addressed / the goals outlined in your PIP that haven't been met]. I know this is hard to hear, and I want to give you time to process it.

I have your separation paperwork here, and I want to walk you through what happens next so you leave today with everything you need."
💡 Say "we've made the decision" — not "I've decided" or "the company decided." The plural signals this isn't personal, it's organizational. And say it clearly in the first 30 seconds — do not lead with how difficult this is for you.

Scripts for three types of termination

Type 1: Performance-based (with prior warnings)

When there's documented history
"As we've discussed over the past [timeframe], there have been consistent gaps in [specific area]. We put a performance improvement plan in place on [date] with clear targets. Despite support and time, those targets haven't been met.

We've made the decision to part ways. Today is your last day. I want to thank you for what you've contributed here and handle this transition with as much care as possible."
💡 Reference specific prior conversations and the PIP. This shows the decision is grounded in documented history, not a surprise judgment.

Type 2: Layoff / restructuring (not performance-related)

When it's the role being eliminated, not the person
"I want to be direct with you. As part of a restructuring, we're eliminating [your role / this department]. This is not a reflection of your performance — this decision was made at a company level based on [direction change / budget / team structure].

Your last day will be [date]. I have your severance agreement and will walk you through the details. I'm also committed to supporting your search — including a strong reference."
💡 "Not a reflection of your performance" needs to be said explicitly. People will assume it is unless you state otherwise. Repeat it if needed.

Type 3: Misconduct or policy violation

When the termination is immediate and for cause
"I need to inform you that we're terminating your employment, effective immediately, for [the policy violation / the incident on X date].

This decision is final. HR is prepared to walk you through the specifics of your exit, and [name] will assist you in collecting your belongings. I'm sorry this has reached this point."
⚠️ For immediate terminations, HR should be present. Keep it short, clear, and don't debate. "The decision is final" is a complete sentence.

How to handle every reaction they might have

😡Reaction 1 — Anger or accusations

This is completely unfair. You've had it out for me since day one. I'm going to talk to HR about this.

What to say:I hear that you're upset, and that's completely understandable. You're absolutely welcome to speak with HR — that's your right. What I can tell you is that this decision was made carefully and is based on [documented performance / the policy violation]. I'm not going to argue the merits of the decision right now, but I do want to make sure you have everything you need as you leave today.

Why this works: you acknowledge their emotion without validating the accusation or getting into a debate. You affirm their right to escalate — which reduces the urgency they feel to fight it in the room. Then you redirect to the practical next steps.

😢Reaction 2 — Tears or emotional breakdown

[Silence, crying, visible distress]

What to say:Take a moment. There's no rush. [Give them time.] I know this is a lot to absorb. When you're ready, I'm here to walk you through the next steps and answer any questions I can.

Why this works: silence and space is the only appropriate response to genuine emotional distress. Do not fill the silence with more words, explanations, or attempts to comfort. Give them time, then gently return to the practical conversation when they're ready.

🙏Reaction 3 — Pleading to reconsider

Please — I'll do whatever it takes. Give me one more chance. I can change.

What to say:I genuinely appreciate hearing that, and I can tell you mean it. But this decision has been made and it's final. I think what you're feeling right now is real — and I believe you'd put that effort into the right next thing for you. That's where I hope this energy goes.

Why this works: you validate their sincerity without reopening the decision. "This decision has been made and it's final" removes false hope without being cruel. The redirect to their future is genuinely kind and honest.

🔕Reaction 4 — Complete silence or shutdown

[Nothing. They stare. Nod. Don't respond.]

What to say:I'm going to give you a moment. [Pause.] When you're ready, I'd like to walk you through the practical next steps — your final pay, the separation agreement, and what happens with [benefits/equipment/etc.]. There's no pressure to respond to everything right now. You can take these documents and reach out to HR with questions in the coming days.

Why this works: silence is a valid response to shocking news. Don't force a conversation they're not ready to have. Give them the documents, give them a path to follow up, and let them leave with dignity.

What to do immediately after

Tell the team the same day

Don't let the news spread informally. Within the same day, brief your team directly. Keep it short: "I want to let you know that [Name] is no longer with the company. I'm not able to share details, but I wanted you to hear it from me directly. I'm here if you have questions." Do not explain the reason. Do not disparage the person. Let the team know you'll discuss workload impact separately.

Revoke access immediately

Systems access, building access, and email should be revoked the same day — ideally within an hour of the conversation. This is not punitive. It's standard protocol that protects the company and removes ambiguity about the person's status. Your IT or security team should have this ready before the meeting.

📧
Do not ghost the terminated employee. Send a brief, professional email confirming the conversation, key dates, and how to reach HR for follow-up questions. Even one sentence: "As discussed today, your last day is [date]. Please reach out to [HR contact] for questions about your separation documents or benefits." This protects both parties and signals basic decency.

The 5 mistakes that make this conversation worse

✗
Burying the news in small talk. "How are you? Did you have a good weekend? So I wanted to chat about..." is cruel. The person shifts from casual conversation to job loss with no transition. Start with the news.
✗
Over-explaining or justifying. The more you explain, the more surface area you create for debate. Say the reason clearly and briefly. Then stop. "We've made the decision" does not need three paragraphs of context.
✗
Saying "this was a difficult decision for me." Making the conversation about your discomfort is unkind. The focus belongs on the person being let go, not on your feelings about letting them go.
✗
Giving false hope. "We might revisit this in six months" or "keep an eye on our postings" when you know this is permanent prolongs pain and damages trust. Be honest about finality.
✗
Not having paperwork ready. Telling someone they're fired and then saying "HR will be in touch about the details" is incomplete. Have the separation agreement, COBRA notice, and final pay details in the room.
