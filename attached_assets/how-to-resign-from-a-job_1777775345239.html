How to Resign from a Job
Gracefully (With Exact Scripts)

The words you say in the first 60 seconds of your resignation will determine how the next 2–4 weeks go. Here's exactly what to say — and how to handle everything they might say back.

Most people rehearse their resignation in the shower for two weeks and then walk into the actual conversation completely unprepared for the one thing they should have seen coming: their manager's response.

The words you planned perfectly in your head evaporate. You say something vague. You get asked a question you didn't expect. You end up explaining yourself more than you meant to, or worse — entertaining a counter-offer you had no intention of accepting.

This guide gives you the exact script for the opening, the three most likely responses your manager will give and what to say to each, and a clear framework for handling the hardest part — the counter-offer — without damaging the relationship you've spent years building.

Before the conversation — what to prepare

The conversation itself takes 10–20 minutes. The preparation takes 30. Here's what you need to have decided before you walk into that room.

Know your last day before you speak

The first question most managers ask is "when is your last day?" If you don't have a clear answer, you lose control of the conversation immediately. Decide your end date before you go in. Standard notice is 2 weeks. If you have a good relationship and want to leave well, 3–4 weeks is generous and appreciated. Don't offer more than 4 weeks — it rarely benefits you and often complicates things.

Decide in advance: are you open to a counter-offer?

This is the most important decision you'll make before the conversation. If your resignation is about money and only money, and the right number would genuinely make you stay — be honest with yourself about that. But if you've already committed somewhere else, or if the reasons you're leaving go beyond compensation, decide firmly that you're not open to a counter-offer before you walk in. Once you're in the room, the emotional pull of a manager you respect offering you more money is very difficult to resist without pre-commitment.

Keep your reason simple and unassailable

You don't owe your employer a detailed explanation. The more detail you give, the more surface area you create for them to argue, negotiate, or guilt you. The strongest resignation reasons are ones that cannot be fixed: "this opportunity is a direction I need to explore," "this is a significant career change," "it's the right move for my family right now." These are honest and complete. You don't need to say more.

⚠️
Don't resign on a Monday or Friday. Monday means you have to spend the whole week in the awkward aftermath before the weekend break. Friday means your manager has the weekend to process it and come back Monday with a full negotiation plan. Tuesday or Wednesday morning is ideal — gives both of you the rest of the week to process, plan the transition, and reach equilibrium before the weekend.

The exact opening words to use

The opening is everything. The first 30 seconds sets the tone for the entire conversation. Here's what works and why.

Opening script — warm and direct Works for most managers
"Hey, do you have a few minutes? There's something important I want to share with you.

I've decided to resign from my position. I've accepted an offer at [company / in a new direction], and I'm planning for my last day to be [date].

I wanted to tell you directly and in person rather than over email. Working here has genuinely meant a lot to me, and I want to make this transition as smooth as possible for you and the team."
💡 Say all of this before they respond. Don't pause after "I've decided to resign" — keep going. A pause there invites an immediate emotional reaction that's harder to manage.
Opening script — if you're close to your manager When you have a strong personal relationship
"I need to share something with you, and I want you to hear it from me first.

I've made the decision to resign. I've accepted an opportunity that I genuinely couldn't turn down — it's [brief honest reason: a different direction / significantly better compensation / a role that aligns with where I want to go].

This was not an easy decision, and part of what made it hard is that I've really valued working with you. My last day would be [date]. I'm fully committed to making the handover as complete as possible."
💡 The phrase "I want you to hear it from me first" signals respect and gives them a moment to feel valued before processing the news.

The biggest mistake people make is over-explaining. Every additional sentence you say is another opportunity for them to find a thread to pull on. Say what needs to be said. Then stop.

How to handle every response they might give

Your manager will fall into one of a handful of patterns. Here's how to handle each one without getting derailed.

😔Response 1 — Disappointment or guilt

I'm really disappointed. I thought you were happy here. This is a real surprise.

What to say:I hear that, and I understand. I have been genuinely happy here — that's part of what makes this decision hard. This isn't about anything wrong with this place or the team. It's about where I need to go next for my own growth. I'm really glad for the time I've been here.

Why this works: you validate their feeling without taking on guilt. You affirm the relationship without reversing the decision. The key is the phrase "where I need to go next" — it signals the decision is about your path, not their failure.

💰Response 2 — Immediate counter-offer attempt

Before you go any further — is this about money? Because I think we can do something about that.

What to say:I really appreciate that, and I want to be honest with you — compensation is part of it, but it's not the whole picture. This is also about [the role itself / the direction / what I want to build toward]. I've thought about this carefully, and I've made my decision. I'd love to spend the rest of our time today focusing on how to make the handover as good as possible.

Why this works: you don't dismiss their offer rudely, but you redirect firmly. "I've made my decision" is a complete sentence and doesn't require justification. Pivoting to the handover signals you're done discussing the resignation itself.

😠Response 3 — Anger or coldness

Fine. I'll need you to wrap up your projects by end of week. HR will be in touch.

What to say:I understand, and I'm sorry this is the reaction. I want you to know that I'm committed to making this as clean as possible — I'll have a full handover document ready by [date] and I'll make myself available for any questions during the transition. I hope over time this lands better.

Why this works: you don't escalate, you don't apologize for leaving, and you leave the door open for the relationship to recover. The phrase "I hope over time this lands better" is gracious without being grovelling.

🙏Response 4 — They ask you to reconsider

I'd really like you to take some time before making this final. Can we talk again in a few days?

What to say:I really appreciate you saying that — it genuinely means something. I've already taken a lot of time with this decision, and I'm confident it's the right one. I don't want to give you false hope that this might change. But I'd love to use the time we do have to make this transition excellent.

Why this works: "I don't want to give you false hope" is kind and clear. It treats them as an adult and closes the door without slamming it.

The counter-offer — how to say no without burning the bridge

If you've decided you're not accepting a counter-offer, this is the hardest moment in the conversation. Here is the exact language that works.

Declining a counter-offer — direct but warm When you're not open to staying
"I'm genuinely moved that you'd do that, and I want you to know it doesn't go unnoticed.

But I've already committed to [the other company / this path], and I think the right thing — both professionally and personally — is to honour that commitment. Accepting a counter-offer at this point wouldn't feel right to me.

What I can commit to is making this the cleanest transition we've ever done. Can we talk about what that looks like?"
💡 "Wouldn't feel right to me" is powerful because it makes the decision about your integrity, not about their offer being insufficient. It's harder to argue with.
📊
The 18-month rule. Research consistently shows that 87% of people who accept counter-offers leave within 18 months anyway — because the underlying reasons they wanted to leave rarely get fixed. A pay rise doesn't change a bad culture, a misaligned career path, or a relationship with a manager that isn't working. If you were ready to leave, a counter-offer is usually just a delay.

The 5 most common mistakes people make when resigning

  • ✗
    Over-explaining your reasons. Every additional detail is a thread they can pull. "I need to explore a different direction" is complete. You don't need to explain what direction, or why this company couldn't provide it, or what specifically was missing.
  • ✗
    Resigning via email when you can do it in person. Email resigns are almost always taken worse. The impersonality feels disrespectful, even when that's not your intention. If you're remote, a video call is the minimum.
  • ✗
    Using the resignation conversation to give feedback. "One of the reasons I'm leaving is that I've felt undervalued" might feel honest, but this is not the right moment. It turns a graceful exit into a difficult conversation. Save feedback for an exit interview if you want to give it at all.
  • ✗
    Going in without a last day in mind. "I was thinking sometime in the next few weeks" gives them control of your timeline. Know your last day before you speak, state it clearly, and let them respond.
  • ✗
    Telling colleagues before your manager. If your manager finds out from someone else, the conversation becomes about the breach of trust rather than the resignation itself. Tell your manager first, always.

What to do after the conversation

The verbal conversation is step one. Here's what to do in the next 24 hours to make sure it's handled correctly.

Send a formal resignation letter the same day

Even if you had the conversation in person, follow it up with a brief written resignation that day — email is fine. Keep it short: one paragraph confirming your resignation, your last day, and your commitment to a good handover. This creates a paper trail, protects you legally, and signals professionalism.

Resignation email template
Subject: Resignation — [Your Name]

Hi [Manager's name],

Following our conversation today, I'm writing to formally confirm my resignation from [role title] at [company name], effective [last day].

I'm committed to a thorough handover and will do everything I can to make this transition smooth for you and the team. I'll have a full handover document ready by [date] and I'm happy to be available for questions during the notice period.

It's been a genuine privilege working here. Thank you for the opportunity.

[Your name]
💡 Keep it under 150 words. Brevity signals confidence and professionalism. Long resignation emails are usually a sign of guilt or over-explanation.

Prepare a handover document

Nothing leaves a better lasting impression than a genuinely thorough handover. Document your current projects, their status, the key people involved, any deadlines, and where all the relevant files live. This is what people remember about you when they think back on your time there — not the resignation itself, but how you left.
